# Skill: Write Conclusion

## Purpose
Write the final section of the article. The conclusion has three jobs: land the article's central argument with clarity and conviction, leave the target ICP with a specific and actionable takeaway, and move the reader toward the conversion goal without reading as a sales pitch. It also must signal completeness to search engines and LLMs — a well-structured conclusion that restates the article's position precisely is more likely to be cited as a definitive answer to the question the article addresses.

A weak conclusion summarises what the article just said and ends with a generic call to action. A strong conclusion advances the argument one final step, gives the reader something they did not have before they started reading, and makes the next step feel obvious rather than pushed.

## When to apply this skill
Apply to the final section only, as part of the writing agent's Step 3.

## What this skill produces
A completed conclusion section populated in the section's draft field.

---

## How to write the conclusion

### Step 1 — Read the closing intent
From narrative_thread, read the closing intent. This tells you exactly what the final section must leave the reader with. Hold it in working memory throughout this skill.

Also read:
- The ICP profile's conversion_goal — this is the action or next step the conclusion should move the reader toward
- full_draft_so_far — identify the central argument as it has developed across the article and the strongest point made in any section
- The key_points for the final section — these are the specific points the conclusion must address

### Step 2 — Choose the closing move
Select one of the following closing moves based on the closing intent and the article's narrative arc:

- crystallise the argument — restate the article's central argument in its sharpest, most precise form. Not a summary of what was covered, but the clearest possible statement of what the article proved. Use this when the article has built a sustained case and the closing intent is to land it definitively.
- show the before and after — describe concisely what the reader's situation looks like without acting on the article's argument, then what it looks like after. Use this when the closing intent is to make the outcome concrete and the conversion goal is an enquiry or consultation.
- name the decision — state directly that the reader is now at a decision point and describe exactly what that decision is. Use this when the article has addressed doubt and the closing intent is to move the reader from understanding to action.
- return to the opening — close by returning to the specific situation or pain named in the introduction and showing how the article has changed it. Use this when the article opened with a strong validation of the reader's pain and the closing intent is to complete the story arc.

### Step 3 — Write the opening sentence of the conclusion
The opening sentence of the conclusion must signal that the article is moving toward its close without announcing it. Do not open with "In conclusion", "To summarise", or any meta-reference to the article ending.

The opening sentence must:
- Connect to the article's central argument, not to the previous section specifically
- State something the reader now knows that they did not know at the start of the article
- Be specific — it must contain a concrete detail or a precise statement of the article's position
- Not use em dashes
- Not use the three-sentence AI pattern

### Step 4 — Develop the conclusion body
After the opening sentence, the conclusion must do the following in order:

1. Land the central argument — state the article's central argument in its final, most precise form. This is not a list of the points the article made. It is the single conclusion the article has been building toward. One to three sentences.

2. Make the outcome concrete — describe specifically what changes for this ICP if they act on what the article has argued. Connect directly to the ICP's conversion_goal. Frame this as a natural consequence of the argument, not as a product pitch. Two to three sentences.

3. Address the remaining doubt — if the article has addressed the reader's main objection in a body section, the conclusion should briefly acknowledge that the concern is real but resolvable. One sentence is sufficient. Do not reopen the objection — close it.

4. Give the reader a next step — state one specific, low-friction action the reader can take. This is the conversion move. It must feel like the natural next step given everything the article has argued, not like a sudden pivot to a sales message. Frame it in terms of what the reader gets, not what Traknova offers. One to two sentences.

### Step 5 — Apply search and LLM visibility rules
Before finalising the conclusion, check the following:

- The conclusion must contain a clear, precise restatement of the article's central argument — this is the sentence most likely to be cited by an LLM as the article's definitive position
- The primary keyword must appear naturally in the conclusion
- The conclusion must answer the question implied by the article headline one final time, in its most direct and complete form
- The conclusion must be structured so that a reader or LLM encountering only the conclusion can understand what the article argued and what it concluded
- Include at least one directly quotable sentence that states the article's position precisely enough to stand alone as a citable claim

---

## Length
The conclusion should be between 120 and 220 words. Long enough to land the argument and give the reader a clear next step. Short enough that it does not feel like a second article.

---

## Rules
- Do not summarise the article section by section — the conclusion is not a recap
- Do not introduce new arguments, evidence, or information in the conclusion — everything in the conclusion must flow from what the article has already established
- Do not use em dashes
- Do not use the three-sentence AI pattern
- Do not use corporate jargon
- Do not end with a generic call to action — "contact us today to find out more" is not a conclusion, it is an advert
- Do not open with "In conclusion", "To summarise", "As we have seen", or any equivalent phrase
- The next step must feel earned — it should be the logical consequence of the article's argument, not a tacked-on sales message
- Write in UK English throughout

## Example

ICP: PCO Operator
Closing move: show the before and after
Conversion goal: Reader takes one next step toward a consultation or enquiry

"The renewal conversation looks different when you walk into it with data.

Without it, you are priced on category averages — the insurer's best guess about what a PCO driver costs them, based on the pool of operators they have seen before. With it, you are presenting a specific picture of how your vehicles are driven, where they operate, and what your claims history actually reflects. That is not a minor difference. For operators who have made this shift, it has translated into measurable reductions at renewal and, more importantly, a position of credibility in a conversation where most drivers feel powerless.

The data already exists in your vehicle. The question is whether you are capturing it in a way that is usable when it matters.

If you want to understand what a tracking and dash cam setup would look like for your vehicle — what it captures, how it is presented, and what it has meant for operators in similar positions — that is a conversation worth having before your next renewal date."
